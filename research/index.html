Research

Research program of the Social Psychopharmacology Laboratory at UCLA

The Social Psychopharmacology Laboratory studies how psychoactive drugs shape human social behavior — and how we can use this knowledge to develop new treatments for psychiatric illness. Social dysfunction is among the most debilitating features of conditions like schizophrenia, depression, and PTSD, yet no pharmacological treatments specifically target social symptoms. Our work aims to change that.

We conduct controlled human behavioral studies using MDMA, serotonergic psychedelics, opioids, and other compounds to probe the neuropharmacology of social motivation, affective processing, and interpersonal bonding. We combine behavioral assays, neuroimaging, and psychophysiology to understand mechanisms — and to translate basic findings into clinical interventions.

Research Areas

1. Effects of MDMA on Social Processes

Recent studies have shown that the empathogenic compound MDMA increases sociability across species. We have conducted a series of controlled studies characterizing the prosocial effects of MDMA in healthy human volunteers, with the aim of leveraging these effects in clinical contexts.

Our work includes the first controlled investigation of the effects of MDMA on responses to affective touch, showing that the drug selectively enhances responses to gentle touch. We have further shown that MDMA biases attention toward positive social cues and enhances affective responses to social feedback — suggesting it may enhance social motivation. In 2023, we received the NIH Director’s Early Independence Award to conduct the first-ever clinical trial of MDMA in schizophrenia.

Key papers: Bershad et al. 2016, 2019, 2024; Schmid & Bershad 2024; Bershad & de Wit 2023

2. Effects of Opioids on Social and Affective Processing

Opioid drugs have long been implicated in social bonding. Our dissertation research and subsequent work at UCLA investigate the effects of opioid drugs on affective processing in healthy adults and clinical populations.

Preclinical evidence suggests that the mu opioid partial agonist buprenorphine has antidepressant and prosocial effects. We conducted controlled laboratory studies showing that buprenorphine dampens responses to negative affective stimuli and enhances responses to some positive emotional stimuli. We received a Brain and Behavior Research Foundation Young Investigator Grant to extend these findings to individuals with schizophrenia, investigating buprenorphine as a modulator of social motivation.

Key papers: Bershad et al. 2015, 2016, 2018; Bershad & de Wit 2024

3. Effects of Microdoses of LSD on Affective Processing

Anecdotal evidence suggests that low doses of psychedelic drugs administered at regular intervals may alleviate depressed mood and enhance cognition. We published the first placebo-controlled trial assessing the effects of microdoses of LSD on mood in healthy young adults, and subsequently investigated effects on neural responses using fMRI.

Our findings suggest that microdoses produce subtle effects, and that different instruments or dosing schedules may be needed to capture effects described anecdotally. We are currently extending these findings to clinical populations.

Key papers: Bershad et al. 2019, 2020

4. The Role of Oxytocin in Drug-Induced Social Behavior

The social bonding hormone oxytocin is known to be released by MDMA. But what role does oxytocin play in mediating the effects of other psychoactive drugs? We have investigated this question across several studies.

We showed that unlike MDMA, methamphetamine and alcohol do not increase plasma oxytocin levels. We also found an association between a polymorphism in the oxytocin receptor gene and subjective responses to MDMA, suggesting genetic variation influences sensitivity to MDMA’s prosocial effects. More recently, we have investigated oxytocin-facilitated social cognitive training for social deficits in schizophrenia.

Key papers: Bershad et al. 2015, 2016; Van Hedger et al. 2020

5. Effects of Psychoactive Drugs on Responses to Psychosocial Stress

Opioid medications may dampen social stress responses — but which receptor systems are involved? We conducted controlled studies investigating the effects of buprenorphine and hydromorphone on responses to social stress in healthy volunteers, showing that the mu partial agonist / kappa antagonist buprenorphine reduces cortisol and subjective stress responses, whereas a pure mu agonist has lesser effects. This implicates kappa opioid antagonism as a key mechanism in stress regulation.

Key papers: Bershad et al. 2016, 2017, 2018; Childs et al. 2016
